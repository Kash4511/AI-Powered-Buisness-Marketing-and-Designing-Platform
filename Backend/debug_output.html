The Six Guiding Principles: Your Roadmap to Federal Compliance4
CHAPTER 01

Risk Management in Sustainable Builds

The U.S. Federal Government has established six core Guiding Principles for Sustainable Federal Buildings to standardize how agencies design, construct, maintain, and operate facilities in ways that increase efficiency, reduce resource consumption, protect occupant health, and mitigate environmental and operational risks.

The U.S. Federal Government has established six core Guiding Principles for Sustainable Federal Buildings to standardize how agencies design, construct, maintain, and operate facilities in ways that increase efficiency, reduce resource consumption, protect occupant health, and mitigate environmental and operational risks. These principles, developed from fundamental sustainable design practices and aligned with the Energy Independence and Security Act (EISA) of 2007 and Executive Order 13834, provide a government-wide framework that eliminates confusion and creates consistency across your portfolio. Understanding these principles is essential for facility managers seeking to secure stakeholder buy-in, accelerate approvals, and demonstrate measurable progress toward sustainability goals. This section provides clear guidance on The Six Guiding Principles: Your Roadmap to Federal Compliance. The discussion focuses on key considerations for The Six Guiding Principles: Your Roadmap to Federal Compliance.

Why These Principles Matter for Your Facility

Federal agencies must comply with statutory requirements for high-performance sustainable buildings. These six principles provide the pathway to demonstrate compliance while generating cost savings, improving operational efficiency, and ensuring your facility remains effective and operational for its entire lifecycle.

Integrated design requires collaboration among architects, engineers, facility managers, and agency leadership from project inception. Principle 3 & 4: Water Conservation and Indoor Environment Quality

The third and fourth principles address resource conservation and occupant wellness. Protecting and conserving water reduces operational costs and environmental impact, while enhancing the indoor environment—through improved air quality, lighting, thermal comfort, and acoustic performance—supports occupant health, productivity, and retention. For government and nonprofit facilities, these principles are particularly critical because they directly affect employee wellness, reduce absenteeism, and create work environments that support mission-critical functions. Communicating these benefits helps facility managers secure funding and stakeholder support for upgrades. Creating Healthier Work Environments

Enhanced indoor environments improve occupant health, wellness, and productivity through better ventilation, daylighting, thermal control, and reduced pollutants. Principle 5 & 6: Material Impact and Building Resilience

The fifth principle requires reducing the environmental impact of materials across the building's lifecycle—from sourcing and manufacturing through installation, maintenance, and end-of-life. This includes selecting sustainable materials, minimizing waste, and considering lifecycle costs. The sixth principle, assessing and considering building resilience, ensures facilities can withstand environmental, operational, and climate-related risks while remaining functional. Together, these principles address long-term sustainability demands by reducing environmental footprint and protecting facility assets from future disruption. For government and nonprofit leaders, resilience is particularly important because facility downtime directly impacts mission delivery and public service. Building Resilience for Long-Term Asset Protection

Resilience assessment identifies vulnerabilities to climate change, natural disasters, infrastructure failures, and other disruptions. Resilient facilities maintain operations during adverse conditions, protecting critical government and nonprofit services. This principle directly supports facility managers' need to secure long-term funding and demonstrate risk management to oversight bodies and stakeholders.

Assessment Pathways and Compliance Documentation8
CHAPTER 05

Assessment Pathways and Compliance Documentation

Federal agencies can demonstrate compliance with the Guiding Principles using two primary pathways: the agency checklists (Appendices A and B) or third-party certification systems (Appendix C). The New Construction and Modernization checklist includes 30 criteria, with 18 designated as core requirements that all buildings must meet, and 12 non-core criteria of which agencies must meet at least 75 percent (9 of 12). The Existing Buildings checklist provides similar flexibility. This structured approach eliminates ambiguity around what sustainability means, streamlines the approval process by providing clear, measurable criteria, and creates transparency in how buildings are assessed and reported to the Office of Management and Budget. For facility managers, these pathways transform sustainability from a vague concept into a documented, auditable compliance process.

Documentation and Reporting for Ongoing Compliance

The Guiding Principles require documented assessment at project completion and periodic reassessment for existing buildings. This documentation demonstrates accountability and progress to the Office of Management and Budget, supporting requests for continued funding and justifying sustainability investments. Clear reporting also helps facility managers communicate achievements to internal stakeholders and the public.
